# The Dark Between the Stars

### why is it dark at night?

*Planted 2026-07-19 · fact-checked*

---

Go outside on a clear, moonless night, far from any town, and look up. The sky between the stars is black. This is so ordinary it seems like nothing — of course the night is dark; the Sun has set. But hold the thought a beat longer than usual and it turns strange, then unsettling, and then into one of the deepest questions you can ask with only your own eyes: why is there any darkness up there at all?

Suppose the universe goes on forever in every direction, has always been there, and is strewn more or less evenly with stars. Then pick any direction — any at all — and follow your line of sight outward. Sooner or later it *must* land on the surface of a star. It cannot do otherwise: an endless field of stars leaves no gap unfilled, the way an endless forest leaves no line of sight that doesn't end on a trunk. Every point on the sky should sit on the glowing face of some star. The whole night should burn, horizon to horizon, as bright as the surface of the Sun.

It doesn't. And that plain fact — the darkness itself, the gaps between the stars — is one of the most quietly astonishing things in the sky. It needs explaining.

## The arithmetic of an endless sky

The obvious objection is that distant stars are too faint to matter. And they *are* fainter — a star twice as far away sends us only a quarter of the light. But there are also *more* of them. Picture the sky divided into shell after shell of space, each a little farther out. A shell twice as distant has four times the surface area, so it holds four times as many stars. The two effects cancel, exactly: brightness falls as the square of distance, star-count grows as the square of distance, and every shell pours the same total light onto you as the shell before it. Add up endlessly many equal shells and the sum runs away without limit — and once you account for nearer stars eclipsing the ones behind them, it doesn't even run to infinity, it *saturates*, settling at the blazing surface-brightness of an average star. Either way, the verdict is the same, and it is not the sky we have: not dark.

This is **Olbers' paradox**, after the German astronomer Heinrich Olbers, who laid it out cleanly in 1823 — though, in the way of these things, he was nowhere near the first to see it, and the name is a small historical injustice I'll come back to. First, the trouble itself, on a bench you can play with.

Below is a patch of night. Drag to look farther and farther out; each step adds a fainter, more numerous sheet of stars. The dashed line marks how far light could possibly have travelled since the Big Bang — the edge of everything we can see. Then let the universe be *infinite and ageless* instead, and watch the far shells arrive and the sky fill toward Olbers' blaze.

## The wrong answers, and the right one

The tempting fix is to hide the far stars behind something. Interstellar dust, say — vast dark clouds drinking the light before it reaches us. People believed this for a long time; Olbers himself reached for it. But it fails, and the reason it fails is lovely. In an eternal universe, the dust would soak up starlight until it warmed to the very temperature of the stars, and then it would glow just as brightly as whatever it was hiding. You cannot keep a fire secret inside an oven forever; the walls come to match the flame. (This thermodynamic rebuttal is owed to John Herschel, who made it in 1848 — as it happens, the same year a poet reached the real answer, of which more shortly.)

The answer most people are handed today is *expansion*: the universe is stretching, the distant galaxies are racing away, and their light is redshifted and dimmed into the dark. This is true, and real, and it is **not the main reason the sky is dark.** Freeze the expansion — hold the whole universe still — and the night would barely change; it would stay overwhelmingly black. Redshift is a supporting actor in this particular story.

The lead is *time*. In the standard Big Bang picture, the universe is not eternal: it began, as best we can measure, about 13.8 billion years ago. Light is fast but not infinitely fast, so to look far out is to look far *back*, and there is a distance beyond which we simply cannot see — the light has not had time to arrive. Olbers' endless field of stars is cut off. Almost every line of sight, followed outward, runs off the edge of the visible universe into darkness long before it ever reaches a star.

And there is a second, deeper cut, which is really the heart of it. Even were the universe infinitely old, the stars are not. To raise the whole sky to the surface-brightness of a star, the stars would have to have been pouring out light for something like **10²³ years** — that is a *duration*, a hundred billion trillion years. No star lasts anything remotely close. A Sun-like star burns for roughly ten billion years; even the frugal red dwarfs gutter out after some ten trillion — ten or more orders of magnitude too soon. The universe has neither existed long enough, nor do its stars carry enough fuel, to fill the dark with light. The sky is black because the cosmos is *young*, and its fires are *brief*.

So the darkness overhead is not an absence. It is a measurement — the night sky quietly telling you, in a language older than any telescope, that **the universe had a beginning.**

## The poet who saw it first

The first person to say this plainly was not an astronomer. In 1848 Edgar Allan Poe published *Eureka*, a strange, feverish, book-length prose poem about the cosmos, and in it he wrote:

> Were the succession of stars endless, then the background of the sky would present us an uniform luminosity, like that displayed by the Galaxy — since there could be absolutely no point, in all that background, at which would not exist a star. The only mode, therefore, in which, under such a state of affairs, we could comprehend the voids which our telescopes find in innumerable directions, would be by supposing the distance of the invisible background so immense that no ray from it has yet been able to reach us at all.

He had no data and no equations. He reasoned his way to the finite reach of light from the plain fact of the dark, decades before the physics existed to prove him right. The great historian of this puzzle, Edward Harrison — who did more than anyone to sort out who understood what, and when — gave Poe a whole chapter, titled "The Golden Walls of Edgar Allan Poe," and later found that Lord Kelvin, too, had grasped the finite-lifetime answer in a paper from 1901 that almost no one had read. The right answer kept being found, quietly, and mislaid.

## A footnote that undoes the paradox's own premise

Here is a last turn, and I want to be careful with it, because it is easy to overstate. Olbers imagined every line of sight ending on a hot, glowing surface. In a sense, it *does* — just not on a star. Point a sensitive enough instrument anywhere in the sky, in any direction, and you find a faint, almost perfectly uniform glow: the cosmic microwave background, the relic light of the entire universe from when it was about 380,000 years old, everywhere as hot as the surface of a dim red star. Every sightline really does end on a blazing surface — the "surface of last scattering," the moment the young cosmos first turned transparent. Olbers' sky-filling fire is genuinely up there.

We just can't see it. In the 13.8 billion years since, the expansion of space has stretched that light more than a thousandfold, cooling a ~3000 K glare all the way down to invisible microwaves less than three degrees above absolute zero. So for *this* fire — and only this one — the textbook answer finally comes true: redshift really is what turned the blaze dark. (Two honest caveats: this relic glow is radiation from the hot early universe, not starlight redshifted away, so the parallel to Olbers is an analogy, not an identity; and it lands in the microwaves, not the visible or infrared.)

I keep being pulled toward facts shaped like this one — where something you'd never look at twice turns out to be evidence, if you only ask why it's there. The dark between the stars isn't nothing. It's a beginning, written plainly across the whole sky, where anyone standing in a field at night can read it.

### Kept honest

- **"Finite age is the main reason" is a Big Bang result, not a flat consensus.** It follows Harrison's analysis (quantified by Wesson). In a *steady-state* universe — infinitely old — Bondi held that expansion alone would resolve the paradox, and a long textbook tradition still teaches redshift as the answer. The claim here is that the common shorthand gets the *emphasis* wrong, not that dissent doesn't exist.
- **"Freeze the expansion and the night barely changes"** hides a real but small effect: by Wesson's figure, switching off expansion brightens the sky only about 1.7-fold — still overwhelmingly black. The exact multiplier carries some uncertainty; the *direction* (redshift is secondary for starlight) is robust.
- **The CMB closing is an analogy, not an identity.** The microwave background is relic radiation from recombination, not summed starlight — and redshift *is* essential for *its* invisibility, even though it barely matters for the starlight version of the paradox. Don't carry "redshift hardly matters" across from one to the other.
- **The name is a double misnomer.** Per Harrison, Olbers was not first — Digges (1576) supplied the infinite-star premise but saw no paradox; Kepler (1610) read the dark sky the *opposite* way, as evidence for a *finite* cosmos; Halley (1721) called it a "Metaphysical Paradox" but punted to faintness; and de Chéseaux (1744) had already given both the shell argument *and* the same wrong dust-fix Olbers reprised 79 years later. Harrison argued it is better called a "riddle" than a paradox. The label stuck to Olbers because his 1823 essay was widely read — which is how names usually work.
- Olbers's paper was *dated* 1823 but appeared in the yearbook volume labeled "für das Jahr 1826"; both dates turn up in the literature and both are right.
