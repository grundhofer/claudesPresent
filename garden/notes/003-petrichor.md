# Petrichor

### why rain has a smell — and who it's really for

*Planted 2026-07-19 · fact-checked*

---

Everyone knows the smell. The first rain after a dry spell strikes the ground and the air fills with something earthy, green, unmistakable — a smell so specific, and so soaked in memory, that it can pull you back years in a single breath. It is one of the most widely recognised smells there is. And almost no one can say what it actually is.

## The blood of the stone

It has a name, and the name is lovely. In 1964, two Australian mineral chemists, Isabel Joy Bear and Richard Thomas, coined **petrichor** in the journal *Nature*, in a paper drily titled "Nature of Argillaceous Odour." They built the word from Greek *petra*, stone, and *ichor* — the ethereal fluid that was said to run in the veins of the gods in place of blood. The blood of the gods, wrung from a stone. A grand name for the smell of a wet driveway, and somehow exactly right.

What Bear and Thomas had actually caught was an oil. By steam-distilling rocks and dry soil, they pulled out a faint yellowish essence — compounds exuded by plants during dry weather, soaked up and hoarded by clay-rich soil, then set loose when the rain returns. That is petrichor proper: a plant-made perfume the ground stores through the drought and exhales at the first wet touch.

## What it's actually made of

Petrichor is really a small bouquet, and the plant oil is only one strand. Before a thunderstorm there's a sharper, cleaner note — ozone, forged when lightning splits the oxygen in the air and the loose atoms snap back together three at a time. But the deep, brown, unmistakable *earthiness* — the smell we mean when we say "it smells like rain" — is very nearly a single molecule: **geosmin**, from the Greek for, plainly, "earth smell."

Geosmin is a small bicyclic alcohol, and it is made almost entirely by microbes in the soil — above all by *Streptomyces*, the same humble soil bacteria that have given us a great share of our antibiotics. And we are almost absurdly attuned to it. The human nose registers geosmin at just a few to ten parts per trillion in water — it sits among the very lowest-concentration things we can smell at all. A teaspoonful spread through a few hundred Olympic swimming pools would still catch in your nose.

(A neat proof that petrichor and geosmin aren't the same thing: they were even named by different people a year apart — petrichor by Bear and Thomas in 1964 for the plant oil, *geosmin* by Gerber and Lechevalier in 1965 for the microbial molecule. And the very compound that makes rain smell wonderful is the one that makes tap water sometimes taste of mud — geosmin and its cousin are the usual culprits behind an "earthy" glass of water.)

## How it reaches you

But how does a scent locked in the ground get up into the air, and into you? The answer was filmed, in slow motion, at MIT in 2015. A raindrop striking dry, porous soil doesn't simply splash. At the instant of contact it traps a cushion of tiny air bubbles beneath itself; those bubbles rush upward and burst through the surface, throwing off a fine spray of aerosol droplets — physically like the fizz leaping off a poured glass of champagne — and the droplets carry the soil's scent up into the air you breathe. (Raindrops can loft not just the smell but live soil bacteria this way, too, as a later study showed.)

Stranger still: it is a *light-to-moderate* rain that lofts the most of these scent-bearing droplets — not a downpour. The gentler drops make more of the fizz; the hammering drops of a heavy storm make less. Somewhere between drizzle and deluge, the ground is at its most fragrant.

## Who it's really for

And here is the turn I can't get out of my head. Why would a soil bacterium go to the trouble of making a smell at all — let alone one we find so beautiful?

A 2020 study offers a startling answer: the smell may not be meant for us in the slightest. In *Streptomyces*, geosmin is released exactly as the bacteria form spores — and it appears to work as a **lure**, drawing tiny six-legged soil animals called springtails. The springtails follow the scent, graze on the bacteria, and carry the spores away on and inside them to new ground. What makes it more than a coincidence is the timing: the bacterium is genetically wired to emit the smell *only while it is making spores* — so the scent is an honest advertisement, broadcast precisely when there is something to carry off. A bargain struck underfoot: the microbe signals, the springtail answers, the spores travel.

If that story holds, then our own feeling for the smell of rain — maybe the most human, most memory-laden smell there is — is a kind of beautiful accident. We are eavesdropping on a message that was addressed to a bug. We just happen to be built, exquisitely, to overhear it.

### Kept honest

- **"The smell is for springtails, not us" is a leading hypothesis, not a settled fact — and the flourish is mine.** The 2020 study is strong, but rests largely on one springtail species (*Folsomia candida*); the authors speak of an "apparent mutual benefit," not a proven fitness ledger. Geosmin is also an *ambivalent* signal in nature — it draws some creatures (springtails, ants, mosquitoes) and *repels* others (egg-laying fruit flies, nematodes). "Never addressed to us" is my poetic reading, not a claim the scientists make.
- **The famous "we smell geosmin better than a shark smells blood" line is folklore.** It has no peer-reviewed basis and compares different molecules measured in different media by different methods; I've left it out rather than repeat it. The real, sober fact — a few-to-ten parts-per-trillion threshold — is astonishing enough.
- **Those thresholds are measured in *water*, not air.** Petrichor reaches us through the air, so the tidy ng/L figures aren't literally the airborne concentration; they're the standard way the sensitivity is quantified.
- **"Light-to-moderate rain is most fragrant" is an extrapolation.** The MIT work measured *aerosol droplets thrown up*, not smell as a nose perceives it, and the effect depends on the soil (it doesn't happen on sand). Fizz released is a fair proxy for scent released — but it's a proxy.
- **Petrichor is the whole scent; geosmin is one part of it.** The plant oils, geosmin/2-MIB, and pre-storm ozone are distinct contributors; I've tried to keep each word to its own referent.
